# German → Spanish Bridges

**Your advantage:** You already understand grammatical concepts that English speakers struggle with. Use this knowledge as a bridge.

---

## What German Prepared You For

### ✅ Grammatical Gender
- German: *der Mann, die Frau, das Kind*
- Spanish: *el hombre, la mujer* (no neuter!)

**Easier in Spanish:** Only 2 genders instead of 3.

### ✅ Verb Conjugation
- German: *ich spreche, du sprichst, er spricht*
- Spanish: *hablo, hablas, habla*

**Similar complexity**, but Spanish patterns are more regular.

### ✅ Formal vs Informal "You"
- German: *du* (informal), *Sie* (formal)
- Spanish: *tú* (informal), *usted* (formal)

**Identical concept**, same social rules.

### ✅ Articles Change for Gender/Number
- German: *der, die, das, den, dem, des...*
- Spanish: *el, la, los, las*

**Easier in Spanish:** No case system (nominative/accusative/dative/genitive).

### ✅ Indefinite Articles Match Gender
- German: *ein Mann* (m), *eine Frau* (f), *ein Kind* (n)
- Spanish: *un hombre* (m), *una mujer* (f) — **no neuter!**

| German | Spanish | Note |
|--------|---------|------|
| ein... | un... | masculine |
| eine... | una... | feminine |
| ~~ein... (neuter)~~ | *(no neuter)* | simplification |

**The session 2 trap:** `une` came out — not Spanish, combination of German *eine* filtered through French *une*. Remember: Spanish indefinite = `un` or `una`. Never `une`.

### ✅ Compound Future Tense
- German: *Ich werde sprechen* (I will speak)
- Spanish: *Voy a hablar* (I'm going to speak)

**Almost identical structure:** auxiliary verb + infinitive

## What's EASIER in Spanish Than German

| Feature | German | Spanish |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Genders** | 3 (m/f/n) | 2 (m/f) |
| **Cases** | 4 (nom/acc/dat/gen) | 0 (no cases!) |
| **Word order** | V2 rule, complex | Fixed SVO |
| **Pronunciation** | Irregular | 100% phonetic |
| **Verb position** | Moves to end in subordinate clauses | Stays fixed |
| **Adjective endings** | Change by case/gender | Only gender/number |

## Key Differences to Watch

### 1. No Cases!

**German:**
- Nominativ: der Mann
- Akkusativ: den Mann
- Dativ: dem Mann
- Genitiv: des Mannes

**Spanish:**
- Just: **el hombre** (always!)

**Implication:** Articles and adjectives don't change by case. Massive simplification.

---

### 2. Subject Dropping (Spanish Does It, German Doesn't)

**German (always use pronoun):**
- **Ich** spreche Deutsch
- **Du** sprichst Englisch
- **Wir** gehen nach Hause

**Spanish (usually drop pronoun):**
- Hablo español *(not: Yo hablo)*
- Hablas inglés *(not: Tú hablas)*
- Vamos a casa *(not: Nosotros vamos)*

**Why?** Verb conjugation makes subject clear.

**When to include in Spanish:**
- Emphasis: **Yo** hablo español (but you don't)
- Clarification: **Él** habla vs **Ella** habla (same form)

---

### 3. Adjective Placement (Opposite!)

**German (adjective BEFORE noun):**
- der **große** Mann
- das **rote** Auto
- die **schöne** Frau

**Spanish (adjective AFTER noun):**
- el hombre **grande**
- el coche **rojo**
- la mujer **bonita**

**From Session 2 (2026-02-20):** `grande café` came out — pure German/English instinct. The fight: your brain knows the *concept* of coffee being large, and German wires it `groß` → `Kaffee`. Spanish flips: `café` → `grande`. Same idea, opposite order.

**Drill pattern:** name the thing first, then its quality.

**Exception:** Numbers, quantity, possessives stay before:
- **Dos** cafés (two coffees)
- **Mi** casa (my house)
- **Mucho** tiempo (much time)

---

### 4. Two "To Be" Verbs (Spanish Only)

**German:**
- *sein* (ich bin, du bist, er ist)

**Spanish:**
- **ser** (permanent/identity): soy, eres, es
- **estar** (temporary/location): estoy, estás, está

**Examples:**
- **Soy** americano — I am American (permanent)
- **Estoy** cansado — I am tired (temporary)
- **Soy** [nombre] — I am [name] (identity)
- **Estoy** en Vegas — I am in Vegas (location)

🇩🇪 **German speakers:** This is NEW. German/English don't split "to be."

---

### 5. Pronunciation (100% Phonetic)

**German:** Many exceptions
- *ie* = long i (*Lied*)
- *ei* = long ai (*Stein*)
- *ch* after a/o/u = *Bach*
- *ch* after i/e = *ich*

**Spanish:** What you see is what you say
- *a* = always "ah"
- *e* = always "eh"
- *i* = always "ee"
- *o* = always "oh"
- *u* = always "oo"

**Implication:** Once you know the rules (3 stress rules, letter sounds), you can pronounce ANY Spanish word correctly on first sight.

## Cognates (German-Spanish)

Many words are similar due to Latin/Greek roots:

| German | Spanish | English |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Familie | familia | family |
| Nation | nación | nation |
| Musik | música | music |
| Problem | problema | problem |
| System | sistema | system |
| Information | información | information |
| Telefon | teléfono | telephone |
| Auto | auto | car |

**Watch out:** Pronunciation is different!
- German *Musik* = moo-ZEEK
- Spanish *música* = MOO-see-kah

## How to Use This Bridge

**When learning Spanish:**

1. **Recognize familiar concepts** — "Oh, this is like German *du* vs *Sie*"
2. **Note the simplifications** — "No cases! This is easier."
3. **Watch for differences** — "Adjective goes AFTER noun here"
4. **Use German as anchor** — "Spanish gender = German gender, minus neuter"

**Don't translate word-for-word.** Use the concepts, not the words.
